Education for Every Child

Across Maasai communities in northern Tanzania, most children still start school later than their peers, walk long distances to get there, and are pulled out long before secondary school begins. This page looks honestly at why — and lays out what Enyuata Maasai Experience is building to change it.

Why This Program Exists

A pastoralist life and a classroom seat shouldn't be a trade-off.

Tanzania has made real progress getting more children into school nationally, but that progress has been uneven. Maasai children — whose families often move with livestock across long distances from the nearest school — remain among the least likely in the country to enroll, and even less likely to make it all the way through.

The reasons aren't a lack of ambition. Research on Maasai communities consistently finds that children and their parents want school to work for them; what's missing is the infrastructure, materials, and flexibility to make attendance realistic alongside herding responsibilities, long distances, and the direct and indirect costs that come with "free" education.

Education for Every Child is Enyuata Maasai Experience's response: practical support that removes the barriers standing between a Maasai child and a full school year — uniforms, books, transport, and the kind of flexible, community-anchored approach that respects rather than replaces pastoralist life.

A note on our data

Enyuata Maasai Experience is a newly established organization. The regional statistics on this page come from published research, UNICEF, and World Bank survey data (cited at the bottom of this page), not from our own program results yet — we have none to report. As our work launches, we'll publish our own enrollment and attendance data here.

The Current Reality

What's keeping Maasai children out of school today

A closer look at the four interlinked pressures shaping school access in the communities we work alongside.

✎

Enrollment starts low and drops further

Studies of Maasai communities in Tanzania estimate primary enrollment at roughly a fifth to a third of school-age children — well below the national average — with girls consistently underrepresented among those who do enroll. For the children who do start, secondary transition rates fall even further.

Source: Maasai Education Foundation; UNICEF Tanzania
⛰

Distance and a pastoralist lifestyle work against attendance

Research on rural Tanzania finds pastoralist households are the least likely of any livelihood group to invest in their children's schooling, largely because herding requires mobility that a fixed school schedule doesn't accommodate — and rural students already travel over 30 minutes to reach school on average.

Sources: ScienceDirect, "Sending children to school"; World Bank Tanzania Panel Survey
💰

"Free" education still carries real costs

Even after Tanzania abolished school fees, families still pay for uniforms, exercise books, transport, and boarding — costs that Human Rights Watch and independent researchers repeatedly identify as the reason the poorest students, including many Maasai families, still can't attend consistently.

Sources: Human Rights Watch; World Bank Gender Equity report
📚

Under-resourced classrooms limit what school can offer

Rural schools frequently lack enough textbooks and trained teachers, with some classrooms sharing one book between ten students — and national reading assessments show only a small share of early-grade students reading at the expected level, meaning attendance alone doesn't guarantee learning.

Source: Powering Potential, Tanzania education system review
Root Causes

Why these patterns persist

None of this happens because Maasai families don't value learning. It happens because of structural gaps that targeted, community-led support can actually close.

Indirect costs of "free" school

Uniforms, exercise books, and transport remain out of reach for many families even without tuition fees.

Long distances to the nearest school

Rural children already travel more than half an hour on average, and pastoralist movement can push that further still.

Herding responsibilities

Children, especially boys, are often needed for livestock work that competes directly with a fixed school day.

Early marriage and pregnancy

For girls, marriage and pregnancy remain leading causes of dropout, particularly from age twelve onward.

Thin school resources

Shortages of textbooks, desks, and trained teachers make it harder for schools to hold students' interest and results.

Language transition

The shift from Swahili to English instruction in secondary school adds a further hurdle for students already behind.

The Way Forward

Our approach to Education for Every Child

A program built around six pillars — each addressing one link in the chain between distance, cost, and a child staying in school long enough to finish.

Pillar 01

Uniforms & School Supplies

Covering the indirect costs — uniforms, exercise books, and stationery — that keep otherwise willing families from enrolling their children.

Pillar 02

Scholarships for Secondary School

Targeted support to help students, especially girls, cross the gap between primary completion and secondary enrollment where dropout is steepest.

Pillar 03

Transport & Boarding Support

Helping cover the cost of transport or nearby boarding for children whose families move seasonally with livestock.

Pillar 04

Books & Classroom Materials

Working with schools to close textbook shortages so every student has the materials to actually learn, not just attend.

Pillar 05

Family & Community Engagement

Working with parents and elders to build flexible arrangements that respect herding responsibilities instead of competing with them.

Pillar 06

Girls' Retention Support

Coordinating with our Women's Dignity Initiative so hygiene access and rights education keep girls in class through adolescence.

Our Roadmap

How we plan to get there

A phased rollout, starting narrow and trusted, then scaling as we learn what works in each community.

01
Phase One — Listen & Pilot

Community entry and first supply distribution

Before committing resources, we sit with parents, elders, and head teachers to understand each school's specific gaps.

  • Partner with 2–3 primary schools in Arusha Region
  • First distribution of uniforms and school supplies
  • Baseline enrollment and attendance data collection
02
Phase Two — Support & Retain

Scholarships, materials, and family engagement

With trust established, we invest in the transition points where dropout is highest — especially the move to secondary school.

  • First scholarship cohort for secondary transition
  • Classroom book and materials support
  • Flexible-schedule pilots with participating schools
03
Phase Three — Scale & Sustain

Transport, boarding, and expansion

Addressing the distance barrier directly, while extending the program to new communities as capacity grows.

  • Transport or boarding support for distant students
  • Teacher-training partnerships
  • Expansion to additional Maasai communities
ⓘ

Where we are right now

Enyuata Maasai Experience is a newly founded nonprofit. The roadmap above describes our plan, not results already achieved. As Phase One launches, we'll replace these projections with real enrollment numbers, real partner schools, and real stories from the children and families in the program — with full transparency about what's working and what needs to change.
